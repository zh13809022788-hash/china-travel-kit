Long-Term Stay in China: A Foreigner's Complete Guide (2026)

Planning to stay in China for 1-6 months? Our guide covers long-term accommodation, visa rules, monthly costs, and daily life tips for slow travelers and expats.

July 9, 2026

Staying in China for a month or more is a completely different experience from a one-week tour. You stop optimizing every day for sightseeing and start living: cooking some of your own meals, finding a neighborhood coffee shop, learning which metro exit is closest to home. This guide is for slow travelers, sabbatical-takers, remote workers, and would-be expats who plan to settle somewhere in China for anywhere from one to six months. It walks through how to stay legally, where to live, what it costs, and how to handle the everyday logistics that a short tourist visit never forces you to think about.

Short Trip vs. Long Stay: What Actually Changes

On a short trip, a hotel absorbs almost every logistical problem for you: it registers you with the police, cleans your room, and sits near the sights. For a long stay, those conveniences either disappear or get expensive, so you trade them for lower daily costs and a real routine. The three things that change most are your legal status (a single tourist entry may not cover the whole period), your housing (nightly hotel rates stop making sense past a few weeks), and your relationship with bureaucracy (address registration, possible visa extensions, and longer-term insurance all become your responsibility). Get those three right and the rest of long-stay life in China is remarkably smooth.

Staying Legally for 1-6 Months

There is no single "long-stay visa" for casual living in China, so most people combine or extend the standard options. Your choice depends on how long you want to stay and whether you have any business reason to be there.

Tourist visa (L) and extensions

The L visa is the default for leisure stays. Depending on your nationality it is issued for 30, 60, or 90 days per entry. If you need a little longer, you can usually apply once for a 30-day extension at the local exit-entry bureau, provided you apply before your current stay expires and can show funds and a place to stay. This comfortably covers stays up to two or three months for many travelers.

Business visa (M) for 3-6 months

If you have any legitimate business connection — meetings, supplier visits, conferences — an M visa is the better fit for longer periods. It is commonly issued with multi-year validity and per-entry stays of 90 to 180 days, which makes multi-month living far less stressful than stacking tourist extensions. It requires an invitation letter from a Chinese company or trade body.

Visa-free transit for short stints

Many nationalities qualify for 240-hour (10-day) visa-free transit when passing through designated cities en route to a third country. This is not a living solution, but it is useful for scouting a city before committing to a longer visa. Check our visa-free and transit checker to see what your passport allows.

Whatever route you take, the golden rule is to plan your exit or extension well before your permitted stay ends. Overstaying carries daily fines and can jeopardize future entries.

Long-Term Accommodation

Where you sleep is the single biggest decision — and cost — of a long stay. There are three realistic options, each with a clear trade-off between price, flexibility, and paperwork.

Serviced apartments

Purpose-built for medium stays, serviced apartments come furnished, include cleaning and utilities, and handle police registration for you. Expect to pay a premium over a bare private rental, but for a one to three-month stay the convenience usually wins. They cluster in business districts of big cities and typically quote monthly rates.

Monthly hotel rates

Many mid-range hotels quietly offer sharply discounted monthly rates, especially outside peak season. You get automatic police registration, daily or weekly housekeeping, and zero lease commitment, at the cost of a smaller space and no kitchen. This is the lowest-friction option if you value flexibility over square meters.

Private apartment rentals

The cheapest per-month option, and the most "local," is renting a private apartment through agents or platforms popular with residents. Landlords usually prefer 6 or 12-month leases and a deposit, and you become responsible for registering your own address at the police station within 24 hours. Best for stays approaching or exceeding six months, or for anyone who wants a kitchen and a real neighborhood.

Wherever you land, register your address with the local police within 24 hours of moving in. Hotels and serviced apartments do this automatically; with a private lease it is on you.

Monthly Cost of Living

China's cost of living varies enormously by city tier. Below are realistic single-person monthly budgets covering rent, food, local transport, and mobile data, assuming a comfortable but not luxurious lifestyle.

City tier Example cities Rent (1BR) Total monthly budget
First tier Shanghai, Shenzhen 7,000-12,000 RMB 12,000-18,000 RMB
New first tier Chengdu, Hangzhou 3,500-6,000 RMB 7,000-11,000 RMB
Smaller / resort Dali, Sanya 2,000-4,000 RMB 5,500-8,500 RMB

The pattern is consistent: rent drives everything. Food, whether you cook or eat at local restaurants, is inexpensive across the board, and local transport rarely exceeds a few hundred RMB a month even with daily metro and ride-hailing use. Budget extra if you rely on imported groceries, international schools, or frequent domestic flights.

Daily Life Essentials

Four systems cover almost everything you will touch day to day. Set them up in your first week and the rest of your stay runs on autopilot.

  • Payments. Alipay and WeChat Pay run daily life, and both now let foreigners link major overseas cards. Set them up before you arrive. Our payment guide covers the full setup and limits.
  • Connectivity. An eSIM gets you online the moment you land; for a multi-month stay you may switch to a local SIM for a domestic number. See the eSIM guides for options.
  • Transport. Metros in big cities are cheap, fast, and signposted in English, and ride-hailing through DiDi works with a foreign card. Add the local transit QR code to your payment app for tap-and-go.
  • Basic healthcare. Big cities have private international clinics with English-speaking staff; public hospitals are cheaper but expect upfront payment and Mandarin. Carry insurance that covers China and keep a small stock of any prescription medication.

Popular Cities for Long Stays

Four cities consistently attract long-stay foreigners, each with a distinct character. Each links to our full city guide for the on-the-ground detail.

Shanghai

China's most international city: the widest English coverage, the deepest expat community, and the most services aimed at foreigners — balanced by the highest rents in the country. Ideal if you want convenience and an easy landing. See the Shanghai city guide.

Chengdu

A relaxed, affordable base in the southwest famous for food, teahouses, and a slower pace, with a growing foreign community and good transport links. See the Chengdu city guide.

Dali

A small lakeside town in Yunnan that has become a magnet for long-stay travelers seeking very low costs, mild weather, and an established community of remote workers and creatives. Best for those who prioritize lifestyle and budget over big-city amenities.

Sanya

China's tropical beach destination on Hainan island, popular for winter stays thanks to warm weather when the rest of the country is cold. Summers are hot and humid. See the Sanya city guide.

Frequently Asked Questions

How long can a foreigner legally stay in China without a residence permit?

It depends on your visa. A tourist (L) visa is usually issued for a 30, 60, or 90-day stay per entry, and some nationalities can extend once at the local exit-entry bureau. A business (M) visa can allow 90 to 180 days per entry over a multi-year validity. Anything beyond roughly six months of continuous living, or any local work, requires a residence permit tied to a work or family visa.

Can foreigners rent a normal apartment in China for a few months?

Yes. Foreigners can rent private apartments, though landlords often prefer 6 or 12-month leases. For 1 to 3 months, serviced apartments and monthly hotel rates are easier because they handle the paperwork. Whatever you rent, you must register your address with the local police within 24 hours of moving in.

Do I need to register with the police if I rent privately?

Yes. Hotels register you automatically, but if you stay in a private apartment or with friends you must register at the local police station within 24 hours of arrival, and again each time you re-enter the country. Bring your passport and the lease or host details. Skipping this can lead to fines.

How much does it cost to live in China for a month as a foreigner?

A comfortable single-person budget ranges from about 6,000 RMB per month in a small city like Dali to 15,000 RMB or more in central Shanghai. Rent is the biggest variable. Food, transport, and mobile data are inexpensive compared with Western cities, so most of your budget goes to accommodation.

Is there a language barrier for longer stays?

Outside major international districts, English is limited. Translation apps, offline maps, and Alipay or WeChat mini-programs cover most daily situations. Learning a handful of Mandarin phrases and how to read a few characters makes a long stay far smoother, but it is not a prerequisite.

Can I use my home health insurance, and do I need local coverage?

China does not require visitors to hold local health insurance, but public hospitals expect upfront payment and most home policies do not pay Chinese providers directly. For a multi-month stay, international travel-medical or expat insurance that covers China is strongly recommended, ideally including private international clinics in big cities.

Can I open a Chinese bank account for a long stay?

It is possible but increasingly paperwork-heavy for non-residents. Many long-stay travelers skip it entirely because Alipay and WeChat Pay now accept major foreign cards for everyday spending. A local account mainly helps if you need to receive RMB locally or pay certain landlords by bank transfer.

What happens if I want to stay longer than my visa allows?

Apply for an extension at the local Public Security Bureau exit-entry office before your current stay expires, or leave and re-enter on a fresh entry if your visa has multiple entries. Overstaying carries a daily fine and can affect future visa applications, so plan your exit or extension well ahead of the expiry date.
