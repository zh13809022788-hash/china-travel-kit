Shanghai City Guide for Foreigners: What to Know Before You Go

A practical Shanghai guide for foreign visitors: getting around, paying, staying connected, neighborhoods, food, and tips for both short trips and longer stays.

July 9, 2026 By ChinaTripBox Editorial Team #shanghai#city guide#china travel#expat

Introduction

Shanghai is where most foreigners have their smoothest first encounter with China. It is the country’s most international city — a place where a century-old riverfront of colonial architecture faces a skyline of supertall towers across the water, and where you can navigate the metro, order dinner, and pay for everything with a phone, all in a single afternoon. For visitors it offers an easy landing; for longer-stay residents it offers the deepest pool of international services in the country. This guide covers the practical essentials for both.

Getting Around

Shanghai’s metro is one of the largest and most efficient in the world, fully signposted in English, and cheap. Add the transit QR code to Alipay or WeChat Pay for tap-and-go entry, or buy single-ride tickets from bilingual machines. Trains run frequently from early morning to around 10:30-11 p.m.

For door-to-door trips, DiDi (China’s ride-hailing app) works with a linked foreign card and spares you explaining directions in Chinese. Street taxis are plentiful and metered; keep small cash for older cabs that lack QR readers. The city is also flat and increasingly bike-friendly, with shared bikes unlockable through app mini-programs.

Where to Stay: Neighborhoods

  • The Bund & People’s Square: central, iconic, and well-connected — best for first-time visitors who want to walk to headline sights.
  • Former French Concession: leafy, low-rise streets full of cafes, boutiques, and restaurants; the favorite of many long-stay foreigners for its atmosphere.
  • Jing’an: polished and central, with malls, offices, and easy metro access — convenient for business travelers.
  • Pudong (Lujiazui): the modern financial district of skyscrapers; great views, more corporate feel.

Money & Connectivity

Set up mobile payment before you arrive — it is the single most useful preparation for Shanghai. Alipay and WeChat Pay both accept major overseas cards now, and QR payment is accepted essentially everywhere. Our broader guides on paying and staying connected go deeper, but the short version: link a card, keep a little cash, and get an eSIM so you are online the moment you land.

Food

Shanghai’s food scene runs from soup dumplings (xiaolongbao) and street breakfast to one of Asia’s most ambitious fine-dining and international-restaurant landscapes. Local specialties lean slightly sweet compared with other regions. Ordering is easy: many places have picture menus or English, and delivery apps let you order to your hotel with in-app payment and translation.

Day Trips

Shanghai is the gateway to the Yangtze Delta’s water towns — Zhujiajiao is the closest, reachable by metro and bus — and high-speed rail puts Hangzhou and Suzhou within about an hour, making both easy day trips or overnight extensions.

Best For

Shanghai is the best Chinese city for travelers who want a soft landing. It is ideal for first-time visitors, business travelers, families, and anyone anxious about language barriers because the metro, airports, hotels, malls, and many restaurants are easier to navigate than in most other cities. It is also the strongest base for longer stays if you want international schools, coworking spaces, familiar services, and a broad foreign community.

The trade-off is that Shanghai can feel less traditionally Chinese on the surface than Beijing, Xi’an, or Chengdu. The city rewards neighborhood walking, food, galleries, cafes, and side trips more than a checklist of ancient monuments. If you want imperial history, pair it with Beijing; if you want scenery and tea culture, add Hangzhou by high-speed rail.

Suggested Itinerary

For a three-day Shanghai trip, start with the Bund, Nanjing Road, People’s Square, and an evening skyline view. Use day two for the former French Concession, Xintiandi, a museum or gallery, and a dinner reservation. Spend day three in Yu Garden, the old city area, and Pudong’s Lujiazui towers, or make a half-day trip to Zhujiajiao if you want a water-town feel without leaving the metro network.

With five to seven days, add slower neighborhood time: Jing’an temples and malls, West Bund museums, riverside cycling, cafe-hopping in the former French Concession, and a high-speed rail day trip to Hangzhou or Suzhou. Shanghai works especially well when you avoid over-scheduling and let the city function as a comfortable base.

Practical Planning Notes

Shanghai has two airports. Pudong is the main international gateway and sits far east of the center; Hongqiao is closer to downtown and integrated with a major train station. Check which airport or rail station you are using before booking hotels or transfers, because cross-city transfers can take longer than expected.

The metro is usually the fastest way to move across town, but late-night returns often require DiDi because many lines stop around 10:30-11 p.m. For payments, Shanghai is one of the easiest places to rely on Alipay or WeChat Pay, though direct foreign-card acceptance is still mostly limited to hotels, luxury retail, and some international restaurants.

Weather affects the experience. Summers are hot and humid, winters are damp and chilly, and spring or autumn are the most comfortable. If rain is forecast, keep museum, mall, cafe, and restaurant plans ready rather than forcing long outdoor walks along the river.

Summary

Shanghai combines genuine convenience for foreigners with world-class transport, food, and culture. For a short trip it is an effortless introduction to urban China; for a longer stay it is the country’s most comfortable landing pad, provided you are prepared for the cost of living.

Frequently Asked Questions

Is Shanghai easy for foreigners who don't speak Chinese?

Shanghai is the easiest major Chinese city for non-Chinese speakers. Metro signage, station announcements, and airport wayfinding are bilingual, and international districts have English-friendly restaurants and services. Elsewhere, translation apps and the mini-programs inside Alipay and WeChat cover ordering, transport, and shopping, so you can function comfortably without Mandarin.

How do I get from Shanghai's airports to the city center?

From Pudong International, the Maglev plus metro or a direct metro ride reaches the center in around an hour, while a taxi takes 45-60 minutes depending on traffic. From Hongqiao, the metro connects to downtown in about 30-40 minutes. Ride-hailing through DiDi works with a foreign card at both airports.

What's the best way to pay for things in Shanghai?

Mobile payment dominates. Set up Alipay or WeChat Pay with your overseas card before arrival and you can pay by QR code almost everywhere. Carry a few hundred RMB in small notes as a backup for older taxis and small vendors. Foreign credit cards work directly only at international hotels and high-end malls.

How many days do you need in Shanghai?

Three to four days covers the headline sights — the Bund, a classic garden, a couple of distinct neighborhoods, and a day trip. A week lets you settle into the city's rhythm, explore the former French Concession slowly, and take in the modern art and dining scene without rushing.

Is Shanghai a good base for a longer stay?

Yes. Shanghai has the widest English coverage, the deepest expat community, the most international services, and excellent transport in China, which makes landing and living straightforward. The trade-off is the highest cost of living in the country, driven mainly by rent.

Is Shanghai safe for tourists?

Very. Violent crime is rare and the city feels safe day and night by international standards. The main precautions are ordinary ones: watch for pickpockets in crowded tourist spots, use licensed taxis or DiDi, and keep digital copies of your passport and visa.
