Beijing City Guide for Foreigners: What to Know Before You Go

A practical Beijing guide for foreign visitors: getting around, top sights, paying, staying connected, neighborhoods, food, and tips for short or longer stays.

July 9, 2026 By ChinaTripBox Editorial Team #beijing#city guide#china travel#expat

Introduction

Beijing is China’s political and cultural heart, and for many travelers it is the reason they come to the country at all. This is the city of the Forbidden City, the Great Wall on its doorstep, imperial temples and vast public squares, threaded through with the narrow hutong alleys of old Beijing. It is grander and more spread out than Shanghai, with a little less English on the street, but its major sights are thoroughly set up for international visitors and its transport is excellent. This guide covers the practical essentials for a smooth visit or a longer stay.

Getting Around

Beijing’s subway is enormous, cheap, and fully bilingual, and it is the fastest way across a city famous for its traffic. Add the transit QR code to Alipay or WeChat Pay, or buy tickets from English-language machines. Because the city is large and its attractions are spread out, plan your days by area to avoid criss-crossing town.

DiDi ride-hailing works with a foreign card and is invaluable for reaching spots away from metro lines. Metered taxis are common; carry small cash for cabs without QR readers. For the Great Wall and other out-of-town sights, a booked car or organized day trip is usually the most efficient option.

Top Sights

  • Forbidden City & Tiananmen Square: the vast imperial palace complex at the city’s core; book tickets in advance as daily numbers are capped.
  • The Great Wall: Mutianyu is the best all-round choice for foreign visitors; allow a full day.
  • Temple of Heaven: a serene imperial altar set in a large park full of locals exercising and socializing.
  • Summer Palace: a lakeside imperial garden, ideal on a clear day.
  • Hutongs: the historic alley neighborhoods around Houhai and Nanluoguxiang — best explored slowly on foot.

Money & Connectivity

As across China, set up mobile payment before you arrive. Alipay and WeChat Pay accept major overseas cards and cover essentially all spending by QR code. Get an eSIM so you are connected on landing, and keep a little cash for backup. Direct foreign-card use is limited to international hotels and high-end retail.

Food

Beijing’s signature dish is Peking duck, best experienced at a dedicated roast-duck restaurant, but the city’s food runs far wider: imperial-style cuisine, hearty northern noodles and dumplings, street snacks in the hutongs, and a growing international dining scene. Picture menus and delivery apps make ordering straightforward.

Neighborhoods to Stay

  • Wangfujing / Dongcheng: central and walkable to the Forbidden City — best for first-timers.
  • Sanlitun (Chaoyang): the nightlife, shopping, and expat hub, with international restaurants.
  • Houhai / hutong areas: atmospheric and historic, with boutique courtyard stays.

Best For

Beijing is best for travelers who want the deepest historical itinerary in China and do not mind a city that requires a little more planning. It suits first-time visitors who care about imperial history, museums, architecture, food, and a Great Wall day trip. It is less ideal if your priority is an easy nightlife-and-shopping city or if you only have one relaxed weekend, because the sights are large, security checks take time, and many top attractions require advance booking.

For families, Beijing works well because the headline attractions are memorable and the metro is predictable, but build in recovery time. The Forbidden City, Summer Palace, and Great Wall can each consume more energy than they look on a map. For solo travelers, the city is safe and straightforward, though English is patchier than in Shanghai and you will use translation apps more often.

Suggested Itinerary

For a three-day Beijing trip, spend day one around Tiananmen Square, the Forbidden City, Jingshan Park, and Wangfujing or a hutong dinner. Use day two for the Great Wall at Mutianyu, returning in time for a simple dinner near your hotel. Day three can cover the Temple of Heaven in the morning and the Summer Palace in the afternoon, or swap one for the Lama Temple and hutongs if you prefer neighborhood wandering.

With five days, slow the pace. Add the National Museum or Capital Museum, a dedicated hutong walk around Shichahai, the 798 Art District, and a flexible food evening. A longer stay also makes it easier to handle ticket availability, weather, and the long distances between sights without turning every day into a forced march.

Practical Planning Notes

Book major sights early, especially the Forbidden City and popular Great Wall transfers. Carry your passport because many attractions and train services require identity checks. Security screening is common at metro stations, public squares, museums, and major tourist areas, so do not schedule tight connections between attractions.

Beijing is seasonal. Spring can be beautiful but windy and dusty; summer brings heat, humidity, and domestic crowds; autumn has the best combination of temperature and visibility; winter is cold but often clear and less crowded. If you are sensitive to air quality, check the forecast and keep indoor alternatives ready.

For transport planning, cluster each day by district. Dongcheng and Xicheng hold many historic sights, Chaoyang is better for nightlife and international dining, and the Great Wall should be treated as a full-day outing rather than an add-on.

Summary

Beijing rewards visitors with the country’s greatest concentration of historical grandeur, backed by excellent transport and visitor infrastructure. It takes a little more planning than Shanghai because of its scale, but for history and culture it is unmatched — and a rewarding, if less international, base for a longer stay.

Frequently Asked Questions

How many days do you need in Beijing?

Four to five days lets you see the essentials without exhausting yourself: the Forbidden City and Tiananmen, a section of the Great Wall as a full-day trip, the Temple of Heaven, the Summer Palace, and a day wandering the hutongs. A week gives breathing room for museums, parks, and the modern art and food scenes.

Which section of the Great Wall should I visit from Beijing?

Mutianyu is the most popular for foreign visitors — restored, scenic, less crowded than Badaling, and equipped with a cable car. Badaling is the closest and busiest. Jinshanling and Jiankou offer wilder, less-restored hiking for the more adventurous. All are day trips from the city.

How do I get from Beijing's airports to the city center?

From Capital International, the Airport Express metro links to the subway network in about 20-30 minutes to reach downtown. From Daxing International, the Airport Express and intercity rail reach the center in roughly 20-30 minutes, while a taxi takes 50-70 minutes given the distance. Ride-hailing via DiDi works at both.

Is Beijing harder for foreigners than Shanghai?

Slightly. Beijing has less pervasive English than Shanghai and a more spread-out layout, but its metro is fully bilingual, the major sights are set up for international visitors, and translation apps plus mobile payment smooth over the rest. It remains very manageable without Mandarin.

What's the best way to pay in Beijing?

Mobile payment, as everywhere in China. Set up Alipay or WeChat Pay with a foreign card before arrival and pay by QR code almost everywhere. Keep a few hundred RMB in small notes for older taxis and small vendors. Direct foreign-card acceptance is limited to international hotels and upscale malls.

When is the best time to visit Beijing?

Autumn (September-October) is widely considered the best: mild, dry, and clear. Spring is pleasant but can be dusty. Summers are hot, humid, and busy; winters are cold but atmospheric and less crowded, and offer clear skies on good days.
