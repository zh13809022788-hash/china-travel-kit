Nanjing City Guide for Foreigners 2026: What to Know Before You Go

Complete 2026 guide to Nanjing for foreign tourists — top attractions like Ming Xiaoling and Niushoushan, Qinhuai River nightlife, local food, metro tips, and practical advice for a smooth visit.

Introduction

Nanjing wears its history differently from Beijing. Where Beijing is imperial and monumental, Nanjing is literary and melancholic — a city where dynasties rose and fell, where the Ming began and where the Republic tried to root itself. It was the capital of six dynasties, the capital of the Ming before Beijing, the capital of the Republic of China, and it carries all those layers visibly in its architecture, its tree-lined boulevards, and its moody riverside evenings.

For foreign visitors in 2026, Nanjing offers something the bigger tourist cities do not: a major Chinese city that still feels like it belongs to its residents rather than to the tourism industry. You do not fight through crowds of selfie sticks at every sight. You can walk the city wall and have long stretches to yourself. The food is excellent, the metro is clean and efficient, and the city is actively improving its international visitor experience — multilingual signage, international payment acceptance, and 240-hour visa-free transit are all in place.

This guide covers everything a foreign traveler needs to plan a trip to Nanjing in 2026.

Getting Around

The Nanjing Metro is your primary tool for getting around. Seven lines cover every major attraction and most of the city. Stations have English names and bilingual signage, and the announcements are in English too. The airport (NKG) connects via Metro Line S1 to Nanjing South Railway Station, where you transfer to the rest of the network. A ride from the airport to Xinjiekou takes about 70 minutes.

For payments: link a foreign credit card to Alipay or WeChat Pay before you arrive, then use the metro QR code function inside either app. You can also buy a physical Yangtze Transit Card at any metro station ticket machine — it costs a small deposit and you top it up at convenience stores or machines.

DiDi works reliably across Nanjing with a foreign card. Taxis are available but drivers do not speak English, so prepare addresses in Chinese. Walking is genuinely pleasant in the central areas — the city is flat, the wutong trees provide shade, and neighborhoods like the old city south of Xinjiekou reward aimless strolling.

Top Attractions

Ming Xiaoling Mausoleum (明孝陵)

The tomb of the Hongwu Emperor, founder of the Ming Dynasty, sits at the foot of Purple Mountain. The approach along the Sacred Way — a winding path lined with massive stone elephants, camels, and mythical beasts — is one of the most atmospheric walks in China. The mausoleum itself is more restrained than Beijing’s Ming Tombs, which makes it feel more authentic and less commercialized. Allow 2-3 hours. Entry is 70 RMB.

Confucius Temple and Qinhuai River (夫子庙·秦淮河)

This is Nanjing’s most famous tourist district, and yes it is crowded in the evenings, but it is also genuinely beautiful. The Confucius Temple itself is worth a quick visit. The real draw is the Qinhuai River after dark — the lantern-lit boats gliding past historic buildings create a scene straight out of a Tang dynasty poem. Take the night cruise (80 RMB, about 40 minutes). The surrounding streets are full of snack stalls and souvenir shops. Go on a weeknight if you can to avoid the worst of the crowds.

Nanjing Massacre Memorial Hall (侵华日军南京大屠杀遇难同胞纪念馆)

This is not a conventional tourist attraction and it is not an easy visit, but it is essential. The memorial is somber, thoughtfully designed, and deeply moving. The museum inside documents one of the worst atrocities of the 20th century with restraint and gravity. Allow 2-3 hours. Entry is free but you must reserve in advance on the official WeChat mini-program. Photography is restricted inside. Be prepared — it stays with you.

Niushoushan Cultural Tourism Zone (牛首山文化旅游区)

The Foding Palace (Buddha’s Summit Palace) at Niushoushan is one of the most extraordinary modern buildings in China — a massive dome built inside a former mine pit, housing a sacred Buddhist relic. The architecture is ambitious: the dome rises from the mountain like a polished shell, and the interior is a vast, hushed space of gold, white marble, and indirect light. Even if you are not interested in Buddhism, the sheer audacity of the design is worth the trip. Niushoushan is about 30 minutes south of the city center by taxi. Entry is 145 RMB. Allow half a day.

Nanjing City Wall (南京城墙)

Nanjing’s city wall is the longest surviving city wall in the world — 35 kilometers of it, built in the Ming Dynasty. You can walk sections of it, and the best stretch for visitors is from Zhonghua Gate (中华门) to the Taiping Gate area. The views from the wall, looking out over the old city and the modern skyline beyond, give you the best sense of Nanjing’s layout and scale. Entry is 50 RMB for the Zhonghua Gate section.

Nanjing Museum (南京博物院)

One of China’s great museums, second only to the Beijing Palace Museum in its collection of imperial artifacts. The permanent collection covers Jiangsu province’s history from prehistoric times through the Ming and Qing dynasties. The highlight is the “Republic of China” exhibition hall, a full-scale recreation of a 1930s Nanjing street with trams, barbershops, and old storefronts — immersive and unexpectedly fun. Entry is free but requires a reservation. Allow 3-4 hours. It is enormous.

Where to Stay

For first-time visitors, the best areas are:

Xinjiekou (新街口) — The central business district. Excellent metro connections, lots of hotel choices from budget to luxury, and surrounded by shopping and restaurants. Best for short visits and first-timers.

Confucius Temple / Qinhuai River area (夫子庙) — More atmospheric and tourist-oriented. Quieter at night along the river. Good if you want to be steps from the nightlife and most attractions. Hotels here tend to be mid-range.

Gulou / Hunan Road (鼓楼·湖南路) — A solid middle ground. Close to the city wall, university area, and several good museums. More local flavor, fewer tourists.

Food

Nanjing’s culinary reputation rests on duck, and it is deserved. The city is the birthplace of salted duck (盐水鸭) — a cold poached duck cured in salt and spices, served sliced. It is delicate, savory, and nothing like the roasted duck of Beijing. Try it at your hotel’s restaurant or a local spot like Han Zhong Men Duck Blood Vermicelli.

Duck blood vermicelli soup (鸭血粉丝汤) is the city’s comfort food staple — duck blood cubes, tofu puffs, vermicelli noodles, and duck gizzards in a rich broth. Li Ji beef pot stickers (李记牛肉锅贴) are legendary: pan-fried until the bottom is crispy, filled with juicy beef and scallions. Xiaolongbao here is excellent too, though the local style has slightly thicker skins than the Shanghai version.

For breakfast, look for street carts selling fan团 (sticky rice rolls) with fried dough sticks and pickled vegetables, or visit a morning noodle shop for a bowl of la chang mian (拉长面) — hand-pulled noodles in broth.

The best food streets are Laomendong (老门东), a restored historic alley with excellent snack stalls, and the streets around Ke Xiang (科巷) market, where locals eat.

Practical Tips

  • Visa: Nanjing offers 240-hour visa-free transit for eligible nationalities, provided you arrive and depart through one of the Jiangsu entry ports (Nanjing airport counts). Check your eligibility before booking.
  • Payments: Alipay and WeChat Pay with a foreign card work at most hotels, major restaurants, and attractions. Keep 200-500 RMB in cash for small vendors and street food. Taxis and the metro both accept mobile payment.
  • Language: The metro and major attractions have English signage. English is not widely spoken outside hotels and tourist sites. Have a translation app ready — Pleco for Chinese text, Google Translate or DeepL for conversation.
  • Getting from the airport: Metro Line S1 from Nanjing Lukou International Airport (NKG) to Nanjing South Station takes about 50 minutes, then transfer to Line 1 or 3 for the city center. A taxi to Xinjiekou costs about 120-150 RMB and takes 45-60 minutes.
  • Internet: China’s internet restrictions apply. Install a VPN or buy an eSIM before arrival. Airlo and Holafly both offer China-specific eSIMs that work with Google services.
  • Best season: Autumn (September-November) is unbeatable. Spring (March-May) is beautiful but can be rainy. Avoid July and August unless you tolerate heat well.

Frequently Asked Questions

How many days do you need in Nanjing?

Three to four days is the sweet spot. Two full days cover the heavyweights — Ming Xiaoling, the Nanjing Massacre Memorial, the Confucius Temple area, and one museum. A third day lets you add Niushoushan (Foding Palace) or a slow wander through the old city. Four days if you want to do both Niushoushan and a day trip to Yangzhou or Zhenjiang.

Is Nanjing more or less foreigner-friendly than Shanghai or Beijing?

Less English on the street than Shanghai, but the metro has bilingual signs and station announcements. Tourist attractions, international hotels, and the airport are well set up for English speakers. Translation apps handle the rest without trouble. The city sees far fewer foreign tourists than Beijing or Shanghai, which means locals are often genuinely curious and helpful when they spot a lost-looking traveler.

What's the best time to visit Nanjing?

March to May and September to November. Spring brings the famous梧桐 (wutong/platanus) trees into fresh green, and the city's boulevards are stunning. Autumn is the most photographed season — Ming Xiaoling's Sacred Way lined with golden and red leaves is unforgettable. Summer is brutally hot and humid. Winter is cold and damp, but the city is quiet and museum visits are comfortable.

How do I get around Nanjing as a foreigner?

The Nanjing Metro covers all major sights and runs from around 6 AM to 11 PM. Buy a Yangtze Transit Card via Alipay and tap your phone at the gates — it works on buses and metro. DiDi (Chinese Uber) works with a foreign card. Taxis are cheap but drivers almost never speak English, so have your destination written in Chinese.

Is Nanjing expensive for tourists?

Noticeably cheaper than Shanghai or Beijing. A decent hotel near Xinjiekou or Confucius Temple runs 300-600 RMB. A solid meal at a local restaurant costs 30-60 RMB per person. Metro rides are 2-6 RMB. Attraction tickets are the main cost — Ming Xiaoling is 70 RMB, Niushoushan is 145 RMB. Overall, budget around 400-600 RMB per day for a comfortable mid-range visit.

What should I eat in Nanjing?

Nanjing's food scene is one of China's most underrated. Must-tries: duck blood vermicelli soup (鸭血粉丝汤), salted duck (盐水鸭) — the city's signature dish, beef pot stickers (牛肉锅贴) from the famous Li Ji brand, and xiaolongbao (soup dumplings). The breakfast scene is stellar — try a fan团 (sticky rice roll) with youtiao from a street cart. The food is less spicy than Sichuan but more flavorful than Shanghai cuisine.
