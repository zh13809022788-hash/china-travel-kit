## Introduction

Guangzhou is the engine room of southern China. For over 2,000 years, it has been a port city — the starting point of the Maritime Silk Road, the gateway through which foreign trade entered China. Today it is China's third-largest city, a manufacturing and logistics powerhouse, and the birthplace of Cantonese cuisine, which many consider the country's finest culinary tradition.

For the foreign traveler in 2026, Guangzhou offers a different China from Beijing's imperial grandeur or Shanghai's cosmopolitan glitter. This is a city of business, of food, of subtropical energy. The Cantonese are proud, practical, and fiercely independent in spirit. The city is chaotic, humid, and at times overwhelming — but it rewards those who lean into it with some of the best eating, most interesting neighborhoods, and warmest people in the country.

## Getting Around

The Guangzhou Metro is one of the best in China — extensive, clean, and well-signed in English. The most useful lines for tourists are Line 1 (running east-west through the city center), Line 2 (north-south), Line 3 (connecting the airport and the central business district), and the APM line (a driverless people mover that runs along the Pearl River waterfront).

For payments, Alipay's metro QR code works on all lines. Taxis are relatively expensive by Chinese standards (flagfall 12 yuan) but still cheap by international measures. DiDi works well. The city is huge and spread out — you will rely on the metro more than in Xi'an or Nanjing.

## Top Attractions

### Canton Tower (广州塔)

The third-tallest tower in China and the iconic symbol of modern Guangzhou. At 600 meters, it dominates the skyline from any vantage point. Go up at sunset for the view over the Pearl River and the sprawling city below. The observation deck is 150 yuan. There is a bubble tram and a skywalk on the top if you are feeling adventurous. The tower is lit up in different colors at night and visible from most of the city.

### Chen Clan Academy (陈家祠)

One of the finest examples of traditional Lingnan architecture in China. Built in the 1890s as a clan temple and academy for the Chen family, it is a masterpiece of Cantonese craftsmanship — the roof ridges are covered in intricate ceramic figurines, the woodcarving is breathtaking, and every surface tells a story from Chinese mythology. The building now houses the Guangdong Folk Art Museum. Entry is 10 yuan. Allow 1.5-2 hours.

### Shamian Island (沙面岛)

A former British and French concession from the colonial era, Shamian is a quiet, tree-shaded island in the middle of the city. It was the site of the Thirteen Factories, the only place where foreign merchants were allowed to trade during much of the Qing Dynasty. Today it is preserved as a historical area with grand colonial buildings, cafes, and studios. A lovely spot for a Sunday afternoon walk — it feels like a different century from the skyscrapers visible across the river.

### Yuexiu Park (越秀公园)

The largest park in the city and home to the Five Rams Statue — the symbol of Guangzhou. The park also contains the Zhenhai Tower (a city museum), a lovely lake with paddle boats, and extensive walking paths. It is free and a good escape from the city's intensity. The nearby Nanyue King Museum (西汉南越王博物馆) is excellent — a rare intact tomb from the 2nd century BC with the jade burial suit of the Nanyue king.

### Beijing Road (北京路)

Guangzhou's premier shopping street and a pedestrian zone that is packed every evening. The unique feature: glass panels in the middle of the street reveal the actual road surfaces from the Song, Ming, and Qing dynasties excavated below. You are walking on 1,000 years of history. The street is surrounded by department stores, restaurants, and food stalls.

### Temple of the Six Banyan Trees (六榕寺)

One of Guangzhou's oldest Buddhist temples, dating back 1,500 years. The nine-story Flower Pagoda is the centerpiece — a stunning octagonal structure that has survived wars and earthquakes. It is a calm, meditative space in the middle of a chaotic city. Entry is 5 yuan.

## Where to Stay

**Tianhe (天河)** — The modern CBD. Highest concentration of international hotels, best metro connections, and most English-friendly. Good for business travelers and first-time visitors who want comfort.

**Liwan / Old City (荔湾)** — The historic heart of Guangzhou. Narrow streets, traditional shophouses, and the best food. Hotels here are mostly budget to mid-range. More atmospheric but less convenient for metro access.

**Shamian Island (沙面)** — Quiet, charming, and unique. Good for couples and those who want a peaceful base. Limited hotel selection but excellent character.

## Food

Cantonese cuisine is widely regarded as the finest in China, and Guangzhou is its undisputed capital.

**Dim sum (点心)** is the ritual. Go in the morning or early afternoon to a proper tea house. Order har gow (shrimp dumplings — the ultimate test of a dim sum chef), siu mai (pork and mushroom dumplings), char siu bao (barbecue pork buns), and cheung fun (rice noodle rolls with shrimp or beef). Drink tea — it is part of the experience.

**White-cut chicken (白切鸡)** is poached chicken served at room temperature with ginger-scallion oil. Simple, pure, and unforgettable. **Roasted goose (烧鹅)** — crispy skin, tender meat, served with plum sauce. **Clay pot rice (煲仔饭)** is rice cooked in a clay pot with Chinese sausage, chicken, or salted fish, with a crispy layer at the bottom.

The best food streets: **Wen De Lu (文明路)** and **Bao Hua Lu (宝华路)** in Liwan district. Also try the restaurants around **Shangxiajiu Pedestrian Street (上下九步行街)**.

## Practical Tips

- **Language:** Cantonese is the local language, but Mandarin is universally understood. English is limited outside business districts and tourist sites.
- **Payments:** Alipay and WeChat Pay with foreign cards work at most restaurants and shops. Street food vendors prefer cash or local payment apps. Keep 300-500 yuan in cash.
- **Climate:** Guangzhou is subtropical and humid. Even in winter (December-February), expect 15-20°C. Summer is oppressively hot and humid. Pack light clothes and an umbrella year-round.
- **Visa:** 240-hour visa-free transit is available for eligible nationalities, provided you arrive and depart through Guangdong entry ports. This includes Hong Kong — you can fly into Guangzhou, take the train to Hong Kong, and fly out, all visa-free within 240 hours.
- **Getting around:** The metro is the best option for most trips. Taxis are relatively expensive. DiDi is reliable.
- **Nearby:** Shenzhen (30 min by train), Hong Kong (50 min by train), Macau (1.5h by ferry).
- **Best season:** October to December. Avoid May to September if possible.
