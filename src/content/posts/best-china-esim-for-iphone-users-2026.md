If you have an iPhone and you are traveling to China, the eSIM question is straightforward: yes, it works. But which one you pick makes a measurable difference in speed, reliability, and whether your apps actually load when you need directions.

I tested four major China eSIM providers on an iPhone 15 Pro in three cities. Here is what held up and what failed.

## The Short Answer

For most iPhone users, **Holafly wins on speed and reliability**. **Airalo is fine for budget travelers** who do not need constant connectivity. **Nomad is the middle ground**. AloSIM works but has the worst coverage in subway stations — the one place you most need data.

The table below breaks down the numbers, but the real-world experience matters more. I will walk through each one.

| Provider | Data | Price (10 days) | Speed | Subway Coverage | Best For |
|----------|------|-----------------|-------|-----------------|----------|
| Holafly | Unlimited | $24 | Fastest | Excellent | Heavy users |
| Nomad | 20 GB | $15 | Good | Good | Balanced use |
| Airalo | 5 GB | $9 | Okay | Spotty | Budget |
| AloSIM | 10 GB | $14 | Good | Poor | General use |

## The Test Setup

I tested all four eSIMs across Beijing, Shanghai, and Guangzhou over two weeks. I used Speedtest by Ookla at five locations per city: a busy shopping street, a subway station underground, a high-speed train, a local restaurant, and a hotel lobby.

Each eSIM was installed as a secondary data line. My primary line (physical SIM) was set to airplane mode to avoid accidental roaming charges. All tests were run on the China Mobile network where possible, which the eSIMs automatically connect to.

## The Winner by Category

### Best Overall: Holafly

Holafly's unlimited data plan at $24 made it the clear winner. Speed tests averaged 52 Mbps in Beijing's CBD, 38 Mbps in Shanghai's Lujiazui, and 31 Mbps in Guangzhou's Tianhe — all fast enough for FaceTime, YouTube, and Google Maps.

The real advantage was the Hong Kong exit node. Because Holafly routes traffic through Hong Kong, it does not trigger China's firewall. This means **you do not need a separate VPN** with Holafly. WhatsApp, Instagram, and Google Maps all just work.

Installation took under 2 minutes from the QR code. The profile downloads cleanly on iPhone 15 Pro, with no configuration changes needed.

### Best Value: Nomad

Nomad's 20 GB for $15 is the best gigabyte-to-dollar ratio. I burned through about 1.8 GB per day with normal use (maps, messages, light browsing), so 20 GB covers 10-11 days comfortably.

Speeds were 15-20% slower than Holafly in most locations, but the connection never dropped completely. The one weakness: switching between cell towers on a moving high-speed train sometimes caused 30-second blackouts.

### Budget Option: Airalo

At $9 for 5 GB, Airalo works for light users. If you plan to rely on hotel WiFi and only use data for maps and messaging, 5 GB might last a week.

The downsides: speeds dropped to 3 Mbps in subway stations, and the connection failed completely in one Guangzhou underground station. For a tourist trying to figure out which exit to take, that is a problem.

### Surprise Option: AloSIM

AloSIM had the most inconsistent experience. Speeds were comparable to Nomad above ground but plummeted to near-zero in subway stations. The installation process also required three QR code scans on iPhone, which is two more than necessary.

## What About Chinese eSIMs?

You will see Chinese eSIMs advertised on travel blogs, usually priced cheaper than Western providers. I tested one (China Mobile Hong Kong eSIM) and returned it. The setup required a Chinese-language app, passport scanning in a specific format, and 48-hour activation. For most foreign iPhone users, the time and friction are not worth the $5 savings.

Stick with Holafly, Nomad, or Airalo. They work within 5 minutes of purchase.

## The One Setting You Need to Change

After installing the eSIM on your iPhone, go to Settings → Cellular → your eSIM line → Network Selection. **Turn off Automatic** and manually select **China Mobile**. This prevents your iPhone from cycling between carriers, which causes the data connection to drop for 10-15 seconds at a time.

This single setting change eliminated the random disconnection problem I had with Airalo and AloSIM.

## Dual SIM Setup: Your Home Line Plus China eSIM

If you use a physical SIM from your home carrier, keep it active and set the China eSIM as your secondary data line. On iPhone, go to Settings → Cellular → Cellular Data and select the eSIM line. Then set your Default Voice Line to your home SIM. This way, calls and texts from your home number still come through, but all data goes through the China eSIM.

There is a catch: if your home carrier detects you are roaming, it may charge you even though data is disabled. To be safe, call your carrier before departure and ask them to block data roaming on your line. Or, if you can live without your home number for the trip, turn off the physical SIM line entirely.
