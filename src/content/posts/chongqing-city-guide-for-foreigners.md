## Introduction

Chongqing is unlike any other city in China. Built on steep hills where the Yangtze and Jialing rivers meet, it is a vertical city — buildings rise on stilts from hillsides, roads spiral over each other, and the 12th-floor entrance of one building opens onto a street that is ground level for another. It is China's largest municipality by population (over 30 million), a city of staggering scale and relentless energy.

For the foreign traveler, Chongqing offers the most visually dramatic urban experience in China. The night view from the south bank of the Yangtze — where the lights of Hongya Cave, the skyscrapers of Jiangbeizui, and the bridges spanning the river create a scene that looks like it was built for a sci-fi film — is unforgettable. The food is legendary. The people are direct and warm. It is chaotic, overwhelming, and absolutely worth the trip.

## Getting Around

The Chongqing Metro is extensive and well-signed in English. The most useful lines for tourists are Line 1 and Line 3. The Line 2 monorail runs along the Jialing River and offers excellent views — sit at the front for the full roller coaster experience as the train climbs and descends through the city.

The Yangtze River cable car (长江索道) is both a tourist attraction and a genuine transport link. It crosses the Yangtze between the north and south banks. Cost is 20 yuan one way. Go around sunset.

Taxis are cheap (flagfall 10 yuan). DiDi works well. Be prepared for traffic — Chongqing's hilly terrain means roads are winding and congestion is common.

## Top Attractions

### Hongya Cave (洪崖洞)

Not an actual cave — a spectacular 11-story stilt-house complex built into the cliff face on the Jialing River. When lit up at night, it looks like something from a fantasy movie — layered traditional buildings with golden lights reflecting on the water. It is crowded and touristy, but the visual impact is undeniable. Go at night. Entry is free.

### Jiefangbei (解放碑)

Chongqing's central shopping district and the heart of the city. The People's Liberation Monument stands at the center of a pedestrian square surrounded by luxury malls. It is the best place to get a sense of the city's energy.

### Yangtze River Night Cruise (两江夜游)

The best way to see Chongqing's spectacular skyline is from the water. The night cruise takes about 45-60 minutes and passes both the Yangtze and Jialing riverfronts. The view of Hongya Cave from the river is the classic Chongqing photo. Tickets are about 150-200 yuan.

### Ciqikou (磁器口)

An ancient town preserved as a shopping and food street. It is very touristy, but the atmosphere is lively and it is a good place to try local snacks and buy souvenirs. The old teahouses along the river are the highlight — sit down for a cup of tea and watch life pass by. Located about 30 minutes from the city center by metro.

### Three Gorges Museum (三峡博物馆)

An excellent museum covering the history of the Three Gorges region, the massive dam project, and the cultural heritage of the Yangtze River. The exhibition on the shipwreck salvaging is unexpectedly fascinating. Located next to the Great Hall of the People (人民大礼堂) — a massive Soviet-style building worth photographing. Entry is free.

### Dazu Rock Carvings (大足石刻) — Day Trip

A UNESCO World Heritage site about 1.5 hours from Chongqing. The Dazu Rock Carvings are over 50,000 statues carved into cliff faces, dating from the 9th to 13th centuries. They are among the finest Buddhist rock art in China, less crowded than the Longmen Grottoes in Luoyang. Allow a full day for the round trip.

## Where to Stay

**Jiefangbei (解放碑)** — Central, convenient, best metro access. Most hotel options. Good for first-time visitors.

**Nan'an District (南岸区)** — Across the Yangtze, with the best views of the skyline. More relaxed. Good for couples and sunset views.

**Chaotianmen (朝天门)** — Where the two rivers meet. Impressive location but less convenient for metro.

## Food

Chongqing hotpot is the star. The broth is a deep red from chili oil, studded with Sichuan peppercorns, and bubbling at your table. You order raw ingredients — beef tripe, pork belly, lotus root, potato slices, tofu, vegetables — and cook them yourself. The dipping sauce is simple: sesame oil, garlic, and a little salt. Enjoying hotpot is a social ritual — locals gather around the pot for hours, eating, drinking beer, and talking loudly.

**Chongqing small noodles (重庆小面)** — the everyday food of the city. Spicy noodles in a simple broth. Sold everywhere for 8-12 yuan. **Spicy chicken (辣子鸡)** — deep-fried chicken pieces buried under a mountain of dried chilies. You pick through the chilies to find the chicken — it's a game and a meal. **Sour fish soup (酸菜鱼)** — for when you need a break from spice, pickled vegetable fish soup is a local favorite.

## Practical Tips

- **Language:** English is limited outside tourist areas. The metro has English signage. Translation apps are essential.
- **Climbing:** The city involves a lot of stairs. Wear comfortable walking shoes. The metro has escalators and elevators.
- **Fog:** Chongqing is known as the "Fog City" — it is overcast more days than not. The dramatic misty atmosphere is part of its charm.
- **Hotpot etiquette:** Order yuanyang if you cannot handle spice. Do not drink the broth directly — it is for cooking ingredients, not for soup.
- **Visa:** 240-hour visa-free transit is available through Chongqing's entry ports.
- **Best season:** March-May and September-November. Avoid July-August.
