## Introduction

Beijing is China's political and cultural heart, and for many travelers it is the reason they come to the country at all. This is the city of the Forbidden City, the Great Wall on its doorstep, imperial temples and vast public squares, threaded through with the narrow hutong alleys of old Beijing. It is grander and more spread out than Shanghai, with a little less English on the street, but its major sights are thoroughly set up for international visitors and its transport is excellent. This guide covers the practical essentials for a smooth visit or a longer stay.

## Getting Around

Beijing's subway is enormous, cheap, and fully bilingual, and it is the fastest way across a city famous for its traffic. Add the transit QR code to Alipay or WeChat Pay, or buy tickets from English-language machines. Because the city is large and its attractions are spread out, plan your days by area to avoid criss-crossing town.

DiDi ride-hailing works with a foreign card and is invaluable for reaching spots away from metro lines. Metered taxis are common; carry small cash for cabs without QR readers. For the Great Wall and other out-of-town sights, a booked car or organized day trip is usually the most efficient option.

## Top Sights

- **Forbidden City & Tiananmen Square:** the vast imperial palace complex at the city's core; book tickets in advance as daily numbers are capped.
- **The Great Wall:** Mutianyu is the best all-round choice for foreign visitors; allow a full day.
- **Temple of Heaven:** a serene imperial altar set in a large park full of locals exercising and socializing.
- **Summer Palace:** a lakeside imperial garden, ideal on a clear day.
- **Hutongs:** the historic alley neighborhoods around Houhai and Nanluoguxiang — best explored slowly on foot.

## Money & Connectivity

As across China, set up mobile payment before you arrive. Alipay and WeChat Pay accept major overseas cards and cover essentially all spending by QR code. Get an eSIM so you are connected on landing, and keep a little cash for backup. Direct foreign-card use is limited to international hotels and high-end retail.

## Food

Beijing's signature dish is Peking duck, best experienced at a dedicated roast-duck restaurant, but the city's food runs far wider: imperial-style cuisine, hearty northern noodles and dumplings, street snacks in the hutongs, and a growing international dining scene. Picture menus and delivery apps make ordering straightforward.

## Neighborhoods to Stay

- **Wangfujing / Dongcheng:** central and walkable to the Forbidden City — best for first-timers.
- **Sanlitun (Chaoyang):** the nightlife, shopping, and expat hub, with international restaurants.
- **Houhai / hutong areas:** atmospheric and historic, with boutique courtyard stays.

## Summary

Beijing rewards visitors with the country's greatest concentration of historical grandeur, backed by excellent transport and visitor infrastructure. It takes a little more planning than Shanghai because of its scale, but for history and culture it is unmatched — and a rewarding, if less international, base for a longer stay.
